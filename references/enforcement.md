# Enforcement — Articles 37–40

How the Data Act is enforced and what penalties apply. Useful for analyses, complaint drafting, and risk discussions.

## Article 37 — Competent authorities

### Verbatim (excerpt)

> "Member States shall designate one or more competent authorities as responsible for the application and enforcement of this Regulation [...]. Member States may establish a new authority or rely on existing authorities."

### Operational notes

- Each member state designates its competent authority or authorities. There may be multiple per member state (e.g., a data-protection authority for personal-data aspects, a sector regulator for sector-specific aspects).
- Where multiple authorities are designated, member states must designate a **data coordinator** to coordinate.
- The Commission maintains a public list of designated authorities. The list is the authoritative reference for any specific member state.

### Skill posture

The skill does not enumerate competent authorities by member state. The lawyer must verify the relevant authority for the member state in question by consulting the Commission's published list.

## Article 38 — Right to lodge a complaint

### Verbatim (excerpt)

> "Without prejudice to any other administrative or judicial remedy, natural and legal persons shall have the right to lodge a complaint, individually or, where relevant, collectively, with the relevant competent authority in the Member State of their habitual residence, place of work or establishment, where they consider that their rights under this Regulation have been infringed [...]."

### Operational notes

- Both natural and legal persons may complain.
- Forum: the member state of habitual residence, place of work, or establishment.
- Complaints can be individual or collective.
- Authorities must inform complainants of the progress and the outcome.

## Article 39 — Penalties

### Operative principle

Member states lay down the rules on penalties applicable to infringements of the Data Act, and take all measures necessary to ensure they are implemented. Penalties must be **effective, proportionate and dissuasive**.

### Caps for specific infringements

- For infringements of **Chapters II, III, V**, penalties may be calibrated taking into account:
  - the nature, gravity, scale and duration of the infringement;
  - any action taken to mitigate;
  - any prior infringements;
  - financial benefits gained or losses avoided;
  - any other aggravating or mitigating factor.
- For **Chapter VI** (DPS switching) infringements, the regulation invokes the higher caps relevant to data-economy regulation (compare GDPR's tiered fines and DMA's caps).
- For infringements concerning **personal data**, supervisory authorities under the GDPR may impose fines under GDPR Article 83 within their competence.

The skill does not give precise euro amounts because they are set in member-state implementing law.

### Drafting implication

Risk assessments should reference both:
- The Data Act's "effective, proportionate, dissuasive" standard, and
- The applicable national implementing penalties.

## Article 40 — Penalties — coordinated approach

The European Data Innovation Board (set up under the Data Governance Act) issues recommendations on penalty levels to encourage coordination across member states. These are not binding but inform proportionality analysis.

## Dispute settlement — Article 10

In addition to complaints to competent authorities, the regulation provides for **certified dispute settlement bodies** for data-sharing disputes between data holders, users, and recipients. These are out-of-court bodies that member states certify against the Article 10 criteria.

### Drafting implication

Pre-contract notices and standard terms may reference the option of dispute settlement under Article 10. The choice between competent authority complaint, dispute-settlement body, and court action is the user's.

## Time limits

### Statute of limitations

The regulation does not set its own statute of limitations for enforcement action. National rules govern.

### Investigation timing

Competent authorities act under their own administrative procedures. The regulation does not impose a time limit on investigations.

## Cross-border enforcement

For matters affecting multiple member states:

- Authorities cooperate via mutual assistance.
- The lead-authority concept under GDPR is not directly transposed; the Data Act relies on the data coordinator within each member state and bilateral cooperation between member states.
- For DPS providers, this can be material — a single switching dispute may engage the customer's habitual establishment and the provider's establishment.

## Damage / civil claims

The Data Act does not establish a sui generis private cause of action. Civil claims arise under member-state law (contract, tort, unfair commercial practices, etc.) on the back of Data Act-derived rights. National civil-procedure rules govern.

For personal data, GDPR Article 82 (right to compensation) operates independently.
