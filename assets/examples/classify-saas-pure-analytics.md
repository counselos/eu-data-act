# Worked Example — Pure SaaS analytics not tied to a specific connected product

A common pattern: a SaaS analytics platform that ingests customer-supplied data (telemetry from various sources, manually uploaded files, API feeds) and provides dashboards, reports, and predictions. The SaaS does not control or configure any specific connected product; it is a generic analytics tool.

---

## Facts (hypothetical)

- A company offers a SaaS analytics platform.
- Enterprise customers subscribe and upload their own data — telemetry from their fleet of equipment, manual files, API feeds.
- The SaaS provides dashboards, reports, predictive analytics.
- The SaaS does not communicate back to the equipment that produced the data and does not configure or update that equipment.
- Customers run their own equipment independently of the SaaS.

## Classification

### Q1 — Connected product (Art. 2(5))

The SaaS provider does not manufacture any connected product. The customer's equipment is its own (and may itself be subject to Article 3(2) disclosure by *that* manufacturer, but that's a separate analysis).

For the SaaS provider as such: **no connected product** in scope of this offering.

### Q2 — Related service (Art. 2(6))

- (2c) Does the SaaS adapt or update the customer's equipment? **NO.** It only ingests and analyses; it does not push commands, configurations, or updates back.

**Conclusion:** the SaaS is **not** a related service.

(Note: per FAQ Q10 last paragraph and Recital 17, "standalone aftermarket services such as auxiliary consulting, analytics and financial services" are excluded from the related-service definition. A pure analytics platform falls into this exclusion.)

### Q3 — DPS (Art. 2(8))

- (3a) Provided to a customer (Art. 2(30)). **YES** — subscription contract with each enterprise.
- (3b) Ubiquitous + on-demand network access. **YES.**
- (3c) Shared, scalable, elastic resources. **YES** (multi-tenant cloud).
- (3d) Rapid provisioning / release. **YES.**

**Conclusion:** the SaaS is a **DPS.**

### Q4 — Overlap

Q2 NO, Q3 YES. **Chapter VI only.**

There is no Data Act access right to the customer's data flowing through the SaaS for the SaaS provider's customers under Chapter II — Chapter II governs connected-product data and related-service data, neither of which is in scope here. Chapter VI governs DPS switching / portability.

(If the customer's data uploaded to the SaaS includes connected-product data the customer obtained from a different data holder, that data is the customer's data — it does not become part of a Chapter II flow administered by the SaaS provider.)

### Q5 — Article 31 carve-outs

Multi-tenant SaaS sold across the catalogue → Art. 31(1) does not apply. Not a non-production beta → Art. 31(2) does not apply.

### Q6 — Article 7 SME

[As applicable.]

### Q7 — Sectoral overlay

If the SaaS is offered to financial entities, DORA may apply. If offered to NIS2-essential entities, NIS2 may apply.

## Important nuance — exportable data scope

Under Art. 2(38), exportable data covers the customer's input + output data + metadata, directly or indirectly generated by the customer's use of the SaaS.

- Customer-uploaded source data → in scope.
- Reports the customer generated using the SaaS → in scope.
- Customer's tenant configuration metadata → in scope.
- The SaaS provider's IP (the analytics models, the internal benchmarks) → out of scope.

The Schedule [Y] of the Article 25 contract should specify the categories above as in-scope, and Schedule [Z] should list provider-internal-functioning data exempted under Art. 25(2)(f).

## Open questions the memo flags

- Does the SaaS in fact send any commands or updates back to any customer equipment? If yes, re-run Q2 — it may be a related service after all.
- Is the SaaS sold to financial entities (DORA) or NIS2 essential entities? If yes, sectoral exit / switching obligations may stack on top of Article 25.
- Does the customer have AI Act obligations (e.g., where the SaaS is identified as a high-risk AI system or has a transparency obligation)? Out of scope here, but flag.
