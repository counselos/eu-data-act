# Worked Example — Non-Defensible Article 4(8) Trade-Secret Refusal

The same wafer-inspection scenario as `trade-secret-refusal-defensible.md`, with procedural and substantive defects that make the refusal non-defensible.

The example illustrates each of the common defects flagged in `references/trade-secret-ladder.md` and `assets/decision-trees/trade-secret-refusal.md`.

---

## Same facts as the defensible example

(See `trade-secret-refusal-defensible.md` for the underlying scenario.)

The competitor of the manufacturer requests access to the calibration-profile data under Article 4(1).

## Defect 1 — Step 1 skipped

The manufacturer does **not** propose Article 4(6) measures. It treats the request as a trade-secret matter and immediately drafts a refusal letter.

**Why this fails.** Article 4(6) is mandatory before any withholding or refusal. The default position is disclosure with confidentiality measures (Recital 31). Skipping Step 1 is procedurally defective and exposes the manufacturer to an Article 38 complaint that will probably succeed.

## Defect 2 — Generic identification of trade-secret data

The refusal letter says "the data contains trade-secret-protected information" without identifying which specific data category is trade-secret-protected.

**Why this fails.** Article 4(6) requires the data holder to "identify the data which are protected as trade secrets, including in the relevant metadata." Generic identification does not satisfy this and does not give the user a meaningful basis to evaluate or contest the position.

## Defect 3 — Conclusory substantiation

The refusal letter asserts: "disclosure would cause serious economic damage to the manufacturer." It does not address:

- Enforceability in third countries.
- Specific level of confidentiality of the data category.
- Uniqueness and novelty.
- Cybersecurity impact.
- Why the loss is **irreparable** as well as serious.

**Why this fails.** Art. 4(8) requires substantiation "on the basis of objective elements." A conclusory assertion does not meet this. Recital 31 specifies that "serious economic damage implies serious and irreparable economic loss" — the irreparability element is missing.

## Defect 4 — Blanket refusal across all inspection data

The refusal letter purports to refuse access to all inspection data generated by the competitor's tool, including operational measurement data unrelated to the calibration profile.

**Why this fails.** Article 4(8) refers to "the specific data in question" and "case-by-case basis." A blanket category-wide refusal is not permitted. The competitor is entitled to operational measurement data subject to standard confidentiality measures.

## Defect 5 — No competent-authority notification

The manufacturer does not notify the competent authority designated under Article 37.

**Why this fails.** Article 4(8) sentence 3 requires notification on refusal. Failure is itself an infringement of the Data Act and a separate ground for an Article 38 complaint.

## Defect 6 — Refusal extends to GDPR data-subject rights

The refusal letter purports to refuse the competitor's request for personal data of which the competitor's directors are data subjects.

**Why this fails.** Recital 31 final sentence: "The exceptions to data access rights in this Regulation should not in any case limit the right of access and right to data portability of data subjects under Regulation (EU) 2016/679."

## Defect 7 — Refusal letter cites the FAQ as binding authority

The letter says "the Commission has confirmed in FAQ Q20 that this category of data is protected" without flagging that the FAQ is non-authoritative.

**Why this fails.** The FAQ explicitly states it is not authoritative. Reliance on the FAQ in a refusal letter as if binding misrepresents the legal basis.

## What happens next

The competitor:

- Lodges an Article 38 complaint with the competent authority.
- Refers the matter to a certified dispute-settlement body under Art. 10.
- Considers a court action.

The competent authority:

- Investigates the procedural defects.
- May order the manufacturer to disclose subject to Article 4(6) measures, having found that Step 1 was not attempted.
- May find that the blanket scope of refusal is itself an infringement.
- May initiate enforcement under Article 39.

## How to fix

Re-run the matter following the Skill's `assets/decision-trees/trade-secret-refusal.md`:

1. Identify trade-secret-protected data with specificity.
2. Propose proportionate measures under Article 4(6).
3. If no agreement, withhold / suspend in writing under Article 4(7) — with substantiation and competent-authority notification.
4. Only if the threshold of Article 4(8) is met (serious AND irreparable economic loss, on objective elements, case by case), issue a refusal letter scoped to the specific data category — with competent-authority notification.
5. Always preserve GDPR data-subject rights.
